\chapter{The Schr\"odinger Equation}

\section{The Hamiltonian and the Energy Basis}
\label{sec_ham_and_energy}

Let's get right into it: the time evolution of a quantum system is determined by the Schr\"odinger equation,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq_schrodinger}
\boxed{
i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} \ket{\psi (t)} = \hat{H}(t) \ket{\psi (t)}.
}
\end{equation}
There's a lot to unpack here, though.  First of all, notice that this is a differential equation for the state $\ket{\psi (t)}$, and I've now explicitly put in the time dependence of the state; from this point on, we should probably assume all states will be time dependent.  

Second, we've introduced a new operator:  the Hamiltonian $\hat{H}(t)$.  This is the quantum operator corresponding to the classical Hamiltonian -- the total energy of the system, kinetic energy plus potential energy:\footnote{You might remember from your classical mechanics course that the Hamiltonian isn't always equal to the total energy of the system, but we won't have to worry about that in this book.}
\begin{equation}
H = T + U.
\end{equation}
The Hamiltonian operator is by far the most important operator in quantum mechanics, and not just because of its appearance in the Schr\"odinger equation.  So let's dig a little deeper into it.

A big part of our job going forward in the book will be to determine the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the Hamiltonian operator; we'll write the eigenvalue equation as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq_eee}
\boxed{
\hat{H} \ket{E_n} = E_n \ket{E_n}.
}
\end{equation}
The notation here is a little different than for other operators; I've written the eigenvalues as $E_n$ (where $n$ is an index -- there will usually be more than one eigenvalue) and the eigenkets as $\ket{E_n}$.  That's because the Hamiltonian corresponds to the observable energy:  when we measure $H$, we're really measuring the total energy of the system.  The eigenvalues $E_n$ are often called the \emph{allowed energies}.  Much of quantum mechanics at this stage is a pursuit of those allowed energies for different systems.

Since it represents an observable, the Hamiltonian must be a Hermitian operator.  As I mentioned back in Section \ref{sec_hermitian_operators}, the eigenvalues of a Hermitian operator are real -- which is nice since they're energies. Furthermore, the eigenstates are orthonormal,
\begin{equation}
\braket{E_m}{E_n} = \delta_{mn},
\end{equation}
and complete, meaning \emph{any} state can be written as a superposition of them,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq_psi_energy_basis}
\ket{\psi(t)} = c_1 \ket{E_1} + c_2 \ket{E_2} + c_3 \ket{E_3} + \cdots = \sum_n c_n (t) \, \ket{E_n(t)}.
\end{equation}

If the Hamiltonian is \emph{time independent}, then we'd expect the eigenstates $\ket{E_n}$ to also be time independent.  But that doesn't mean the state $\ket{\psi(t)}$ doesn't evolve in time -- in general it will.  In this case all the time dependence is carried in the coefficients $c_n(t)$, and it turns out we can solve the Schr\"odinger equation for any system -- as long as the Hamiltonian of that system doesn't depend on time.

The trick is to write the state in the energy basis, as in equation (\ref{eq_psi_energy_basis}).  If we plug that into the Schr\"odinger equation, we get
\[
i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} \left( \sum_n c_n(t) \ket{E_n} \right) = \hat{H} \left(\sum_n c_n(t) \ket{E_n} \right).
\]
Now we can move the derivative into the sum, and since the eigenstate $\ket{E_n}$ doesn't depend on time here, it will only act on the coefficients $c_n(t)$.  On the right hand side, we can move the Hamiltonian operator right beside the state, since the coefficients are just numbers, giving
\[
i\hbar \sum_n \frac{dc_n}{dt} \ket{E_n} = \sum_n c_n(t) \hat{H} \ket{E_n}.
\]
Of course, we know that $\hat{H} \ket{E_n} = E_n \ket{E_n}$ -- it's the eigenstate of the operator, after all, and the reason why we are using the energy basis here in the first place, so we have
\[
i\hbar \sum_n \frac{dc_n}{dt} \ket{E_n} = \sum_n c_n(t) E_n \ket{E_n}.
\]

We need to solve this equation for the unknown coefficients $c_n(t)$, but they're buried in the sums.  To get rid of the sums and isolate the coefficeints, we take the inner product with the eigenstate $\ket{E_m}$ -- notice the index is $m$ rather than $n$:
\[
\bra{E_m} \left( i\hbar \sum_n \frac{dc_n}{dt} \ket{E_n}  \right) = \bra{E_m} \left( \sum_n c_n(t) E_n \ket{E_n} \right).
\]
Now let the bra act on the ket on each side to get
\[
i\hbar \sum_n \frac{dc_n}{dt} \braket{E_m}{E_n} = \sum_n c_n(t) E_n \braket{E_m}{E_n}.
\]
But since the eigenstates are orthonormal, we have
\[
\braket{E_m}{E_n} = \delta_{mn}.
\]
This is a little tricky to see at first, but that Kronecker delta $\delta_{mn}$ will collapse the sum -- every term in the sum will end up being zero except for the $m$th one.  So the equation reduces to 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq_schro_ode}
i\hbar \frac{dc_m}{dt} = c_m E_m.
\end{equation}
Finally, this is differential equation we can readily solve; separating and integrating gives the solution as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq_schro_coeff}
c_m (t) = c_{m,0} e^{-i E_m t / \hbar}
\end{equation}
(try it yourself!), where $c_{m,0} = c_m(0)$.

We have now the solution to the Schr\"odinger equation in cases where the Hamiltonian is time independent.  There's just one last thing: it's customary to drop the naught in the initial value of the coefficient $c_{m,0}$  and just write it as $c_m$ (or, as I'll write it in a moment, $c_n$ -- the index letter doesn't matter). This might seem a little confusing, since $c_m$ could also stand for the value at time $t$, $c_m(t)$, but it'll turn out we'll never write it that way -- $c_m$ will henceforth always mean the initial value.

Anyway, here we go:  the solution to the Schr\"odinger equation, written in the energy basis, is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq_schro_sol}
\boxed{
\ket{\psi(t)} = \sum_n c_n e^{-iE_n t / \hbar} \ket{E_n}.
}
\end{equation}
And, just to be clear, we're not \emph{really} done -- to finish this off, we need to know the energy eigenstates $\ket{E_n}$ (which will depend on the Hamiltonian $\hat{H}$) and all of the coefficients $c_n = c_n(0)$ (which will depend on the initial conditions).

\section{Stationary States}

Although we'll be spending the next several chapters solving the energy eigenvalue equation -- really, we'll be doing it \emph{a lot} -- let's pretend for a moment that we already know what the eigenvalues $E_n$ and eigenstates $\ket{E_n}$ are, and suppose that our system just happens to initially be in the state $n=1$ state:
\[
\ket{\psi(0)} = \ket{E_1}.
\]
This is the energy eigenstate, so we know that a measurement of the energy on the system at time $t=0$ will yield $E = E_1$ (whatever that value happens to be). Question:  what will a measurement of the energy get at a later time $t$?

This is what the Schr\"odinger equation does:  given an initial condition, it tells us how the state evolves in time, and the answer is apparently given by equation (\ref{eq_schro_sol}).  We still have to figure out all the coefficients $c_n$, but we can do that by using our initial condition.  Plugging in $t=0$ into equation (\ref{eq_schro_sol}) gives
\[
\ket{\psi(0)} = \sum_n c_n \ket{E_n}.
\]
But we know $\ket{\psi(0)} = \ket{E_1}$, so we must have $c_1 = 1$ and every other $c_n = 0$.  Then we just go back to the full solution and plug in our known coefficients $c_n$ to get the first part of our answer: the state at a later time $t$ is given by
\[
\ket{\psi(t)} = e^{-iE_1 t/\hbar} \ket{E_1}.
\]

Let's take a look at this state.  Notice that the only difference from the initial state is the presence of the exponential factor in front -- but that's an overall phase factor, which we've said doesn't physically change the state.  So has anything really changed in time?  Not really; if we measure the energy, the probability we'll get $E_1$ is
\[
\mathcal{P}_{E_1} = | \braket{E_1}{\psi(t)} |^2 = | e^{-iE_1t/\hbar} \braket{E_1}{E_1}|^2 = |e^{-iE_1t/\hbar}|^2 = 1,
\]
or 100\% -- the same as at $t=0$.  

In fact, \emph{any} measurement of \emph{any} observable -- which I'll write as $A$ -- is time-independent:
\[
\mathcal{P}_{a_n} = |\braket{a_n}{\psi(t)} |^2 = | e^{-iE_1t/\hbar} \braket{a_n}{E_1}|^2 = |\braket{a_n}{E_1}|^2.
\]
This probability obviously depends on the observable $A$ that we're measuring, but not on time at all.  States for which there is no measurable time evolution -- that is, every measurement we can make has no time dependence -- are called \emph{stationary states}.  The energy eigenstates of a system are always stationary states.

\section{Superposition States}

What if our initial state was 
\[
\ket{\psi(0)} = c_1 \ket{E_1} + c_2 \ket{E_2}
\]
instead?  This is called a \emph{superposition state} for obvious reasons.  In this case equation (\ref{eq_schro_sol}), after applying our initial conditions, gives us the state at a later time as
\[
\ket{\psi(t)} = c_1 e^{-iE_1t/\hbar}\ket{E_1} + c_2 e^{-iE_2t/\hbar} \ket{E_2}.
\]
You can try using the initial conditions to show this if you like, but notice the pattern that's forming:  if we write our initial state in the energy basis, then all we have to do to evolve that state forward in time is to ``tack on'' the exponential time factors $e^{-iE_nt/\hbar}$ to each appropriate term.  

If we measure the energy of our system at some later time $t$, notice that the probabilities stay constant in time:
\[
\mathcal{P}_{E_1} = | \braket{E_1}{\psi(t)}|^2 = |c_1|^2, \quad \text{and} \quad \mathcal{P}_{E_2} = |c_2|^2.
\]
This is \emph{always} the case if the Hamiltonian is time independent -- energy probabilities don't change.

What about some other observable $A$?  Then it depends.  If the operators $\hat{A}$ and $\hat{H}$ commute, then they share eigenstates -- so the states $\ket{E_n}$ are also eigenstates of $\hat{A}$ and the probabilities will be constant as well.

However, if $\hat{A}$ and $\hat{H}$ \emph{don't} commute, they won't share eigenstates.  However, since the energy eigenstates are complete, we could always write the eigenstates of $\hat{A}$ as superpositions of the energy eigenstates,
\begin{equation}
\ket{a_m} = \sum_n \alpha_n \ket{E_n}.
\end{equation}
Then we can calculate measurement probabilities $\mathcal{P}_{a_n}$, but they would end up varying with time.

This last part is a little confusing, so let's go through the process with a simple example.

\begin{example}[Time Evolution]

Consider some generic two-state system -- we'll do some more specific and physical examples in the next chapter -- with a Hamiltonian given by
\[
\hat{H} \to \begin{pmatrix}
E_1 & 0 \\ 0 & E_2
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Notice that, as a diagonal matrix, $\hat{H}$ is written here in its own basis (the energy basis), and that means the diagonal terms $E_1$ and $E_2$ are the possible energies we'd measure.

Suppose we have a second observable $\hat{A}$ which we'll write in the energy basis as
\[
\hat{A} \to \begin{pmatrix}
0 & a \\ a & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

We're going to explore this system in detail, and it starts with finding the eigenvalues and eigenstates of these two operators.  Luckily we already have the answer for the Hamiltonian:  the eigenvalues are the allowed energies $E_1$ and $E_2$, and the associated eigenstates are just the kets $\ket{E_1}$ and $\ket{E_2}$, which we'll write in matrix notation as
\[
\ket{E_1} \to \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{and} \quad \ket{E_2} \to \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}.
\]

The operator $\hat{A}$ requires more work.  We have to solve the characteristic equation, $\det |\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I} | = 0$, where $\lambda$ represents the possible eigenvalues.  Let's work it out:
\[
\begin{vmatrix} -\lambda & a \\ a & -\lambda \end{vmatrix} = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda^2 - a^2 = 0,
\]
so $\lambda = \pm a$.  To find the eigenstates, we need to write down the eigenvalue equation,
\[
\hat{A} \ket{a} = \lambda \ket{A},
\]
and plug in our eigenvalues, one at a time.  For $\lambda = +a$, we can write
\[
\hat{A} \ket{+a} = +a \ket{+a}
\]
and solve for the ket $\ket{+a}$.  This is easy to do in matrix notation, where the eigenvalue equation becomes
\[
\begin{pmatrix}
0 & a \\ a & 0
\end{pmatrix} 
\begin{pmatrix}
\alpha \\ \beta
\end{pmatrix} 
= a \begin{pmatrix}
\alpha \\ \beta
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Multiplying out the left hand side and equating the top rows of each side (or the bottom rows) gives $\beta = \alpha$, so we know our eigenstate is 
\[
\begin{pmatrix}
\alpha \\ \alpha
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
But how do we get $\alpha$?  Remember, every state must be normalized, so we must also have
\[
\braket{+a}{+a} = 1 \to \begin{pmatrix}
\alpha & \alpha
\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
\alpha \\ \alpha
\end{pmatrix} = 1,
\]
or $\alpha = 1/\sqrt{2}$.  Our eigenstate $\ket{+a}$ is
\[
\ket{+a} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \ket{E_1} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \ket{E_2} \to \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{pmatrix}
1 \\ 1
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Similarly -- try it on your own -- the eigenstate $\ket{-a}$, corresponding to eigenvalue $-a$, is
\[
\ket{-a} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \ket{E_1} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \ket{E_2} \to \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{pmatrix}
1 \\ -1
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

Well, that's been a lot of work already, and we haven't even really answered any questions.  Let's answer some now.  We'll set up our system so that at time $t=0$ it's in the state
\[
\ket{\psi(0)} = \ket{+a}.
\]

 \begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
 \item A measurement of the observable $A$ is made at $t=0$.  What are the possible results and probabilities of each?

This one is easy since we start out in an eigenstate of $\hat{A}$ -- we'll measure $A = +a$ 100\% of time, and never measure $A = -a$.

 \item  A measurement of the energy $E$ is made at $t=0$.  What are the possible results and probabilities of each?

The possibilities are the energy eigenvalues -- the allowed energies -- which are $E_1$ and $E_2$.  The probability of measuring $E = E_1$ can be calculated in the usual way,
\[
\mathcal{P}_{E_1} = | \braket{E_1}{\psi(0)}|^2 = | \braket{E_1}{+a}|^2 \to \left| \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1/\sqrt{2} \\ 1/\sqrt{2} \end{pmatrix} \right|^2 = \frac{1}{2}.
\]
Of course, that means we must have $\mathcal{P}_{E_2} = 1/2$ as well.  So we have a 50/50 chance of measuring each allowed energy.

\item What is the state at a later time?

To write out the state at a later time, we have to make two steps:  first, write the initial state in the energy basis.  We already have this from above,
\[
\ket{+a} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \ket{E_1} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \ket{E_2}.
\]
Second, tack on the appropriate exponential time factors, giving
\[
\ket{\psi(t)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} e^{-iE_1t/\hbar} \ket{E_1} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} e^{-iE_2t/\hbar}  \ket{E_2}.
\]
That's it!

\item A measurement of the energy $E$ is made at a time $t$.  What are the possible results and probabilities of each?

The possible results are still the allowed energies $E_1$ and $E_2$.  What about the probabilities?  Well,
\[
\mathcal{P}_{E_1} = | \braket{E_1}{\psi(t)}|^2 = \left| \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} e^{-iE_1t/\hbar} \right|^2 = \frac{1}{2}.
\]
Then $\mathcal{P}_{E_2} = 1/2$ as well -- but these are the same values we found at $t=0$.  As promised, the energy probabilities are constant in time.

\item A measurement of the observable $A$ is made at a time $t$.  What is the probability of measuring $A = +a$?

Let's make the calculation in matrix notation, but be careful to make sure everything is in the same basis -- the energy basis in this case.  Also, it gets a little messy:
\begin{align*}
\mathcal{P}_{+a} & = | \braket{+a}{\psi(t)} |^2 \\
& = \left| \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\end{pmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{pmatrix} e^{-iE_1t/\hbar} \\ e^{-iE_2t/\hbar} \end{pmatrix} \right|^2 \\
& = \frac{1}{4} \Big| e^{-iE_1t/\hbar} + e^{-iE_2t/\hbar} \Big|^2
\end{align*}
Remember that those absolute value symbols actually mean we have to multiply by the complex conjugate, so
\begin{align*}
\mathcal{P}_{+a} & = \frac{1}{4}\Big( e^{iE_1t/\hbar} + e^{iE_2t/\hbar} \Big) \Big( e^{-iE_1t/\hbar} + e^{-iE_2t/\hbar} \Big) \\
& = \frac{1}{4} \Big( 2 + e^{i(E_1 - E_2)t/\hbar} + e^{i(E_2 - E_1)t/\hbar} \Big) \\
& = \frac{1}{4} \Big( 2 + e^{i\omega t} + e^{-i\omega t} \Big) \\
& = \frac{1}{2} \Big( 1 + \cos(\omega t) \Big),
\end{align*}
where I've defined $\omega \equiv (E_2 - E_1) / \hbar$.  Notice that $\omega$ has the units of angular frequency -- loosely, it tells us how fast the state is changing in time.  Anyway, now we have an interesting situation -- something finally changes in time!  The probability of measuring $A = +a$ varies with time; at $t=0$ it's 100\%, but at time $t=\pi/\omega$ it's actually zero.  At time $t=2\pi/\omega$ it's back up to 100\%, and in fact it oscillates sinusoidally in time as shown in Figure \ref{fig_simple_time_evolution}.

\end{enumerate}

\end{example}

\begin{problem}[Solving the Schr\"odinger Equation]
\label{prob_solve_schro}

In Section \ref{sec_ham_and_energy} we solved the Schr\"odinger equation for the case where the Hamiltonian doesn't depend on time, and we got down to the differential equation (equation \ref{eq_schro_ode})
\[
i \hbar \frac{dc_m}{dt} = c_m E_m.
\]
Solve this equation and show that that the answer is as given in equation (\ref{eq_schro_coeff}).

\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Checking the Solution]
\label{prob_check_schro}

Check our solution to the Schr\"odinger equation (equation \ref{eq_schro_sol}) by explicitly plugging it into the Schr\"odinger equation itself and showing that the right hand side equals the left hand side.

\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Practice 1]
\label{prob_schro_practice1}

Consider the Hamiltonian given by
\[
\hat{H} \to E_0 \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}
\]
where $E_0$ is some real positive constant.  This matrix is written in the basis given by the kets $\ket{q_1}$ and $\ket{q_2}$, which are eigenstates of some operator $\hat{Q}$, and have corresponding eigenvalues $q_1$ and $q_2$.    Initially the system starts out in a state given by
\[
\ket{\psi(0)} = \frac{3}{5} \ket{q_1} + \frac{4}{5} \ket{q_2}.
\]

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]

\item What are the allowed energies of the system?

\item Find the energy eigenstates of the Hamiltonian.

\item If you were to make a measurement of the energy of the system at $t=0$, what are the probabilities of measuring each?

\item If you were to measure $Q$ at time $t = 0$, what are the possible results and probabilities of each?

\item What is the state $\ket{\psi(t)}$ at a later time?

\item If you were to measure $Q$ at a later time $t$, what are the possible results and probabilities of each?

\item What is the expectation value $\avg{A(t)}$ as a function of time?

\end{enumerate}

\end{problem}

\hat{H} \to  \begin{pmatrix} E_0 & 0 & A \\ 0 & E_1 & 0 \\ A & 0 & E_0 \end{pmatrix},
\]
where $E_0$, $E_1$, and $A$ are real positive constants.  This matrix is written in a basis given by the kets $\ket{1}$, $\ket{2}$, and $\ket{3}$.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]

\item Find the energy eigenvalues and eigenstates.

\item Write the Hamiltonian in the energy basis (as a matrix).

\item Suppose the initial state of the system is $\ket{\psi(0)} = \ket{1}$.  What is the probability that the system is in state $\ket{1}$ at time $t$?

\end{enumerate}
